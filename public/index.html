About Me

I am a postdoctoral scholar in economics at the University of Reading, UK. I apply quantitative methods to study economic problems. My research interests include the political-economics of development, measurement of firm productivity and its determinants, and the impact of climate change on household welfare.

Road to Productivity: The Effect of Roads on Manufacturing TFP in India

In this article, we examine the effect of roads on Total Factor Productivity (TFP) in Indian manufacturing. We exploit exogenous variation in the timing and duration of partisan alignment of states with the centre that affect implementation of road projects to identify the impact of roads on manufacturing TFP during 1998-2012. After controlling for endogeneity of road placement, we find that an increase in road density leads to a 0.10-0.34 log point increase in TFP at the industry level or, about a 12 percentage point increase in TFP for each percent increase in road density. We find heterogeneous impact of roads on TFP: smaller firms, incumbents and industries in urban states see higher TFP gains than their respective counterparts. Furthermore, long-differenced results suggest that besides scale-effects, improving road density affects the growth rate of TFP. Placebo tests on our instrument and several robustness checks reinforce our findings.
Kailthya S, Kambhampati U
Working paper, 2018

Infrastructural Development and Firm Efficiency in India (1999-2007)

In this paper, we examine the impact of macroeconomic conditions and firm characteristics on firm efficiency and output in the Indian manufacturing sector. We estimate stochastic frontier production functions for six different industries using level annual data for 1999-2007. Our results indicate that while frontier output showed an overall increase, efficiency levels remained largely stagnant during our study period. We find no positive impact of infrastructural improvements on firm efficiency. The results stay robust to using a synthetic index of macroeconomic variables. In almost all the cases, we find that infrastructural improvements in power generation capacity, electrification of railways, bank expansion or road construction are associated with a rise in frontier output (or production potential of firms) and a decline in firm efficiency (or the actual to potential output ratio). Our analysis suggests that the underlying reason for this puzzle might be a shifting out of production frontiers over time at a pace higher than the rate of increase in output, which is consistent with uneven utilisation of improvements in infrastructure amongst firms.
Hui N, Kailthya S, Kambhampati U
In Review of Development and Change, 2015
